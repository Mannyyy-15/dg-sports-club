What Is Kabaddi? Understanding the Game

For those new to the sport, kabaddi is a contact team game played between two teams of seven players. The objective is simple: raiders cross into the opponent's half, tag as many defenders as possible, and return to their own half â€” all while holding a single breath. Defenders try to tackle the raider and prevent their return.

What makes kabaddi unique among team sports is the individual challenge within a team framework. When a raider enters the opponent's half, it's one against seven â€” a situation that builds extraordinary mental toughness, decision-making ability, and physical courage. No other sport creates this kind of pressure environment so regularly.

The sport has deep roots in Indian culture, with references appearing in the Mahabharata and ancient Tamil literature. Today, it's played competitively in over 30 countries, with India remaining the dominant force. The Pro Kabaddi League, launched in 2014, has transformed the sport's visibility and inspired a new generation of young players â€” including many at our sports academy in Virar.

Basic Raiding Techniques: The Art of Scoring

Raiding is the most exciting aspect of kabaddi â€” and the skill that most young players want to master first. A successful raider combines speed, agility, deception, and courage to score points against the defense.

The Touch and Get Away

The simplest raiding technique involves reaching out to tag a defender with the hand and quickly retreating to the midline. For beginners, this technique teaches:

  • Extension: Reaching far enough to make contact while maintaining balance
  • Speed: Getting in and out of the defense before they can react
  • Awareness: Knowing exactly where all seven defenders are positioned

The Running Hand Touch

A more advanced technique where the raider runs across the defense line, using speed and a sweeping hand movement to tag defenders. This requires:

  • Explosive acceleration from a standing start
  • Lateral movement and body feints to create openings
  • Quick decision-making about which defender to target

The Kick

Kickers use their legs to tag defenders from a distance. The toe kick and the mule kick are popular variations. This technique develops:

  • Balance on one leg while extending the other
  • Timing and precision in targeting
  • Core strength and flexibility

The Dubki

Perhaps the most spectacular kabaddi move â€” the dubki involves ducking under a defender's arms and escaping through a gap in the defense. It requires:

  • Exceptional body awareness and flexibility
  • Courage to go low in a contact situation
  • Split-second timing to exploit the gap

At DG Sports Club, our coaches introduce raiding techniques progressively. Beginners start with basic touch raids and develop more complex techniques as their confidence and physical ability improve. Every technique is taught with emphasis on safety and proper form.

Defensive Skills: The Foundation of Winning

While raiding gets the glory, defense wins championships. A strong defense can neutralize even the most talented raiders, and the best kabaddi teams are built on solid defensive foundations.

The Chain Tackle

The most fundamental defensive technique in kabaddi. Two or more defenders link arms and coordinate their movement to trap the raider. Effective chain tackling requires:

  • Communication: Defenders must coordinate their movements verbally and through physical connection
  • Timing: The tackle must be initiated at the right moment â€” too early and the raider escapes, too late and they've already scored
  • Commitment: Half-hearted tackles result in failed attempts and potential injuries

The Dash

A high-risk, high-reward defensive move where a defender charges at the raider from the side, pushing them out of bounds. The dash develops:

  • Explosive acceleration and power
  • Courage and commitment to full-contact situations
  • Reading the raider's movement patterns

Ankle Hold

A precise technique where a defender grabs the raider's ankle to immobilize them. This requires:

  • Timing and anticipation of the raider's foot placement
  • Grip strength and quick reflexes
  • Lower body strength to maintain the hold

Block and Cover

Positional defense where defenders cover specific zones of the court, creating a wall that limits the raider's movement options. This teaches:

  • Spatial awareness and court positioning
  • Anticipation of the raider's likely movement
  • Team coordination and role understanding

Our kabaddi coaching in Virar places equal emphasis on defensive skills. We believe that a young player who can defend well will always be valuable to any team â€” regardless of their raiding ability.

Fitness Benefits: Building a Complete Athlete

Kabaddi demands an extraordinary range of physical attributes, making it one of the most comprehensive fitness activities available for young athletes. A single training session can develop:

Explosive Power

The sudden bursts of acceleration required for raiding and tackling build fast-twitch muscle fibers â€” the muscles responsible for explosive movements. Research from the Indian Journal of Sports Medicine shows that kabaddi players develop significantly higher lower-body power compared to athletes in non-contact sports.

Agility and Reflexes

Kabaddi requires constant changes of direction, rapid deceleration, and split-second reactions. These demands develop neural pathways that improve agility and reaction time â€” skills that transfer directly to football, cricket, athletics, and virtually every other sport.

Cardiovascular Endurance

Despite the stop-start nature of kabaddi, the sport places enormous demands on the cardiovascular system. A raider typically sprints at maximum intensity for 30 seconds, recovers for 2-3 minutes, then sprints again â€” a pattern that develops both aerobic and anaerobic fitness simultaneously.

Core Strength and Stability

The grappling and contact aspects of kabaddi require tremendous core strength. Players develop strong abdominals, obliques, and lower back muscles through the twisting, turning, and wrestling movements inherent in the sport.

Flexibility

Techniques like the dubki, high kicks, and evasive movements require and develop exceptional flexibility. Regular kabaddi training improves range of motion in the hips, shoulders, and spine â€” reducing injury risk in all physical activities.

A study by the Sports Authority of India found that youth kabaddi players scored 15-20% higher on comprehensive fitness assessments compared to peers who participated in single-dimension sports. The reason is simple: kabaddi demands everything from the body, and the body responds by becoming more capable.

FAQs About Kabaddi Training

What is the minimum age to start kabaddi training?

Children can start learning basic kabaddi movements from age 6-7. At this age, training focuses on movement patterns, agility, and fun modified games rather than full-contact practice. At DG Sports Club, our junior kabaddi program begins at age 6 with progressive skill development.

Is kabaddi safe for children?

Yes, when practiced under proper supervision with age-appropriate rules. Youth kabaddi uses modified contact levels, smaller playing areas, and shorter raid times. Our coaches at DG Sports Club prioritize safety through proper technique instruction and progressive training.

Do girls play kabaddi?

Absolutely. Women's kabaddi has grown tremendously, with professional leagues featuring women's teams. India's women's kabaddi team has won multiple Asian Games gold medals. At DG Sports Club, we welcome both boys and girls in our kabaddi program.

What fitness level is needed for kabaddi?

Kabaddi is excellent for building fitness from any starting level. Our training programs develop speed, agility, strength, and endurance progressively. Beginners don't need any specific fitness level â€” just willingness to learn and have fun.
